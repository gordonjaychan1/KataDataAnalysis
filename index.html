Welcome!

This site presents a statistical analysis of all kata performances recorded during the 2024–25 WKF season — covering both the Male and Female Kata categories across the nine Tier 1 tournaments.

This dataset captures every individual performance: which kata was performed, the score received, and whether the athlete won that match.

About the Data

Data was manually collected from official WKF scoring sheets and tournament results. The dataset covers 9 tournaments: the four 2024 Karate1 Premier League events (Paris, Antalya, Cairo, Casablanca), the four 2025 K1 events (Cairo, Hangzhou, Paris, Rabat), and the 2025 World Karate Championships in Abu Dhabi.

Kata are classified into three tiers — Advanced, Intermediate, and Beginner — based on the USA Karate - Official Kata List (see References for more information).

Data Collection

Data was manually collected from official WKF live scoring sheets and publicly available tournament results. Only the average score (Avg Score) is retained — the raw total score has been dropped. All analyses involving scores use the average score only.

Kata Tier System

Kata are classified into three tiers based on technical complexity:

  • Advanced — High-difficulty kata featuring complex techniques, unusual stances, or rare traditional forms. Almost exclusively performed at elite international level. Examples: Suparinpei, Unsu, Ohan, Papuren, Gojushiho Sho.
  • Intermediate — Mid-level kata common at national and continental level, occasionally seen at WKF events. Examples: Sochin, Shisochin, Seienchin, Kanku Dai.
  • Beginner — Lower-difficulty kata rarely seen at this level of competition.

At the K1 Premier League and World Championship level, virtually all performances are Advanced tier kata.

WKF Scoring System

Five judges each assign a score from 0–10. The highest and lowest scores are discarded; the remaining three are averaged to produce the final score. This dataset records only this average score. A score above 8.0 is generally considered competitive at the K1 Premier League level; top finals performances regularly reach 8.4–9.3.

The "Won?" Field

Two athletes compete simultaneously in a head-to-head match. Both perform their kata, and judges determine the winner by raising a flag. Won? records whether the athlete won that match. Win rate across multiple performances indicates how often a given kata choice — or athlete — leads to victory, though it is influenced by many factors beyond the kata itself.

Notes on Interpretation

  • Kata with fewer than ~10 performances may have skewed statistics due to small sample size.
  • A kata with a high average score may simply be performed by a smaller pool of elite specialists — not necessarily "better" than other kata.
  • Male and female events are entirely separate. Do not compare scores across genders.
  • Athletes who competed in only one or two tournaments may have unrepresentative stats.

About Me

I grew up in California and began training in karate at age six. My primary style is Hayashi-ha Shito-ryu, the system I have trained in throughout my life. When I entered university I joined the campus Shotokan karate club, training in Shotokan alongside my Shito-ryu practice — and I now coach that same club.

I have been competing since I was ten years old. Kata has always been the dimension of karate I find most compelling: the depth of technique, the demands of precision under pressure, and the endless pursuit of refinement.

Why I Made This

I studied Management Information Systems in college and took a course on Data Science with Python. The analytical skills from that class mapped directly onto a question I had long wanted to explore: what can the data from elite international kata competition actually tell us?

This project is an attempt to answer that question — to give competitors, coaches, and fans a clearer picture of which kata are chosen at the highest level, how scores compare across athletes, and what patterns emerge over a full WKF season. I hope it serves as a useful resource for anyone with a serious interest in kata.

Methodology

Performance data was manually collected from official WKF scoring sheets and tournament results across all nine events of the 2024–25 season. Python was used as the primary language for data processing and analysis, with the following libraries:

  • Pandas — for structuring, cleaning, and manipulating the dataset
  • NumPy — for numerical computations and descriptive statistics
  • Chart.js — for all interactive charts on this site

Kata were manually classified into Advanced, Intermediate, and Beginner tiers based on technical complexity. See Important Notes for full tier definitions.
