Welcome!

This site presents a statistical analysis of all kata performances recorded during the nine Tier 1 tournaments in the 2024–25 WKF season, covering both the Male and Female Kata categories.

If you are unfamiliar with kata competition, see "Kata Competition Basics" below for a brief breakdown.

Kata Competition Basics Click to open. +

Two athletes compete in a head-to-head match. One performs their kata first, then the next athlete performs. Each judge on a panel of seven assigns a score from 0–10. The highest and lowest scores are discarded; the remaining five are averaged to produce the final score. Refer to the linked YouTube video at the bottom of the Home page for an example.

At Tier 1 tournaments, athletes are split into eight groups of four competitors each (if there are fewer than 32 total competitors, certain groups will have only three athletes). Each group runs a round robin between all competitors, and the athlete with the most wins advances to the bracket stage. In the bracket stage, the eight group winners are placed into an 8-person single-elimination bracket and compete in three rounds until the bracket concludes. After the bracket concludes, two repechage ("second-chance") matches are held: athletes who were eliminated earlier by the two finalists get another chance and compete for the two bronze medals (4 athletes total).

At the World Championships, the top two athletes from each group progressed to the bracket stage, resulting in a 16-person single-elimination bracket.

Kata Tier System

Kata are classified into three tiers (Advanced, Intermediate, and Beginner) based on the USA Karate - Official Kata List (see Figure App-2 in References for more information).

In competition, a competitor may only perform a kata from their tier or lower. For example, a competitor in an Intermediate Kata Division may not perform an Advanced kata; they must perform an Intermediate or Beginner kata.

At Tier 1 WKF Events, all divisions are Advanced, meaning any kata may be performed (Advanced, Intermediate, or Beginner).

  • Advanced: High-difficulty kata featuring complex techniques, unusual stances, or rare traditional forms. Almost exclusively performed at elite international level. Examples: Suparinpei, Unsu, Ohan, Papuren, Gojushiho Sho.
  • Intermediate: Mid-level kata common at national and continental level, occasionally seen at WKF events. Examples: Shisochin, Seienchin, Kanku Dai.
  • Beginner: Lower-difficulty kata rarely seen at this level of competition.

Tiers are broad bands, not a strict ranking. Difficulty varies widely within each tier, and the bands overlap: the most demanding Intermediate kata are harder than the least demanding Advanced kata. The majority of Advanced kata were performed, while only a small minority of Intermediate kata were performed, indicating that, when choosing Intermediate kata, elite athletes only chose the best of them. See Figure K-10 to see which Advanced and Intermediate kata were and were not performed.

Each curve shows how the kata in one tier spread across difficulty. Where the curves overlap, some Intermediate kata are more advanced than some Advanced kata.

About the Data

Data was manually collected from official WKF live scoring sheets and publicly available tournament results. The dataset covers 9 tournaments: the four 2024 Karate1 Premier League events (Paris, Antalya, Cairo, Casablanca), the four 2025 K1 events (Cairo, Hangzhou, Paris, Rabat), and the 2025 World Karate Championships in Cairo.

Only the average score (Avg Score) is retained; the raw total score has been dropped. All analyses involving scores use the average score only.

WKF Scoring System

Seven judges each assign a score from 0–10. The highest and lowest scores are discarded; the remaining five are averaged to produce the final score. This dataset records only this average score. A score above 8.0 is generally considered competitive at the K1 Premier League level; top finals performances regularly reach 8.5–9.0.

The "Won?" Field

Two athletes compete simultaneously in a head-to-head match. Both perform their kata, and judges determine the winner by raising a flag. Won? records whether the athlete won that match. Win rate across multiple performances indicates how often a given kata choice (or athlete) leads to victory, though it is influenced by many factors beyond the kata itself.

Notes on Interpretation

  • Kata with fewer than ~10 performances may have skewed statistics due to small sample size.
  • A kata with a high average score may simply be performed by a smaller pool of elite specialists, not necessarily "better" than other kata.
  • Male and female events are entirely separate. Do not compare scores across genders.
  • Athletes who competed in only one or two tournaments may have unrepresentative stats.

About Me

My name is Gordon Jay Chan, and I began training in karate at age six. I train Hayashi-ha Shito-ryu, but when I entered university I joined the campus Shotokan Karate club, training in Shotokan alongside my Shito-ryu practice, and I now coach that same club.

I have been competing since I was ten years old. Kata has always been the dimension of karate I find most compelling: the depth of technique, the demands of precision under pressure, and the endless pursuit of refinement.

Why I Made This

I've always been interested in kata competition. Why do people choose certain kata? Which kata is that athlete's best? Are there trends in kata selection and performance? I was also curious about the hard numbers behind each kata: median score, max score, and so on. That way, the next time someone claimed "Actually, I think Ohan Dai was more popular than Unsu," I'd finally have real data instead of guesswork. At university I studied Management Information Systems and took a course on Data Science with Python, so I applied those analytical skills to elite international kata competition.

This project is a resource for me, and hopefully many other curious people, to answer as many of those questions as possible. I want a clearer picture of which kata are chosen at the highest level, how scores compare across athletes and tournaments, and everything in between. I hope it helps others explore the trends and questions they have too!

Methodology

I manually collected and compiled performance data from sportdata.org, the official online platform used by the WKF for scoring sheets and tournament results. Python was used as the primary language for data processing and analysis, with the following libraries:

  • Pandas: for structuring, cleaning, and manipulating the dataset
  • NumPy: for numerical computations and descriptive statistics

The interactive charts on this site are built with Chart.js, a JavaScript library.
