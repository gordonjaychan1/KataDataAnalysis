2024–25 WKF Season — Kata Analysis

Welcome!

This site presents a statistical analysis of all kata performances recorded during the nine Tier 1 tournaments in the 2024–25 WKF season — covering both the Male and Female Kata categories.

Reading the Tables

All tables are sortable: click any column header to sort ascending or descending. Click again to reverse the sort. Hover over any column header to see a tooltip explaining what it represents.

In detail cards, a small number below a stat value (e.g., 3/42) is a rank: it shows where that entry places among all entries for that stat. (T) means the rank is tied with at least one other entry.

Every main table (Kata, Athletes, Tournaments, Countries) has a pinned Averages row at the bottom showing the mean of each column. Hover over any cell in that row to see a tooltip explaining what the average computes.

Kata are classified into tiers (Advanced, Intermediate, or Beginner) based on the USA Karate Kata List. A colour-coded badge appears next to each kata name. See Figure App-2 in the References tab for the full tier list.

Red Links & Navigation

Any kata name, athlete name, tournament, or country that appears in red is a clickable link. Hovering underlines it; clicking opens a prompt asking if you want to navigate to that entry's detail card.

This works everywhere: inside detail cards, inside tables on the findings tabs, and in the search results. It lets you jump freely between related entries without losing your place; you can always use the ← Back button (bottom-left) to return to where you came from.

How do I jump from an athlete's profile to the kata they performed, or from a kata to the athlete who scored highest?

Male / Female Toggle

The Male / Female buttons in the top-right corner switch every page between the male and female kata datasets. All tables, charts, and stats update instantly, with no reload needed.

Hover over either button to see a tooltip with a full season summary for that gender: total performances, unique kata, athletes, tournaments, and countries.

Figures are labelled by gender so you can cite them precisely: male figures are numbered K-1, A-1, … while the same female figures are numbered FK-1, FA-1, …

About the Data

Data was manually collected from official WKF live scoring sheets and publicly available tournament results. The dataset covers 9 tournaments: the four 2024 Karate1 Premier League events (Paris, Antalya, Cairo, Casablanca), the four 2025 K1 events (Cairo, Hangzhou, Paris, Rabat), and the 2025 World Karate Championships in Cairo.

Only the average score (Avg Score) is retained — the raw total score has been dropped. All analyses involving scores use the average score only.

Kata Tier System

Kata are classified into three tiers — Advanced, Intermediate, and Beginner — based on the USA Karate - Official Kata List (see Figure App-2 in References for more information).

In competition, a competitor may only perform a kata from their tier or lower. For example, a competitor in an Intermediate Kata Division may not perform an Advanced kata; they must perform an Intermediate or Beginner kata.

At Tier 1 WKF Events, all divisions are Advanced, meaning any kata may be performed (Advanced, Intermediate, or Beginner).

  • Advanced — High-difficulty kata featuring complex techniques, unusual stances, or rare traditional forms. Almost exclusively performed at elite international level. Examples: Suparinpei, Unsu, Ohan, Papuren, Gojushiho Sho.
  • Intermediate — Mid-level kata common at national and continental level, occasionally seen at WKF events. Examples: Shisochin, Seienchin, Kanku Dai.
  • Beginner — Lower-difficulty kata rarely seen at this level of competition.

WKF Scoring System

Seven judges each assign a score from 0–10. The highest and lowest scores are discarded; the remaining five are averaged to produce the final score. This dataset records only this average score. A score above 8.0 is generally considered competitive at the K1 Premier League level; top finals performances regularly reach 8.5–9.0.

The "Won?" Field

Two athletes compete simultaneously in a head-to-head match. Both perform their kata, and judges determine the winner by raising a flag. Won? records whether the athlete won that match. Win rate across multiple performances indicates how often a given kata choice — or athlete — leads to victory, though it is influenced by many factors beyond the kata itself.

Notes on Interpretation

  • Kata with fewer than ~10 performances may have skewed statistics due to small sample size.
  • A kata with a high average score may simply be performed by a smaller pool of elite specialists — not necessarily "better" than other kata.
  • Male and female events are entirely separate. Do not compare scores across genders.
  • Athletes who competed in only one or two tournaments may have unrepresentative stats.

About Me

I grew up in California and began training in karate at age six. I train Hayashi-ha Shito-ryu, but when I entered university I joined the campus Shotokan Karate club, training in Shotokan alongside my Shito-ryu practice — and I now coach that same club.

I have been competing since I was ten years old. Kata has always been the dimension of karate I find most compelling: the depth of technique, the demands of precision under pressure, and the endless pursuit of refinement.

Why I Made This

I've always been interested in kata competition and why people choose certain kata and whether there are useful trends in kata selection and performance. Also, I was just curious about what every kata's median score, max score, etc. was. At university, I studied Management Information Systems and took a course on Data Science with Python. To answer my questions, I took the analytical skills from that class and applied them to elite international kata competition.

This project is a resource for me (and hopefully many other curious people) to answer as many questions as I can. I want a clearer picture of which kata are chosen at the highest level, how scores compare across athletes, and what patterns emerge over a full WKF season. I hope it serves as a useful resource, and others find it enjoyable and useful.

Methodology

Performance data was manually collected from official WKF scoring sheets and tournament results across all nine events of the 2024–25 season. Python was used as the primary language for data processing and analysis, with the following libraries:

  • Pandas — for structuring, cleaning, and manipulating the dataset
  • NumPy — for numerical computations and descriptive statistics
  • Chart.js — for all interactive charts on this site
