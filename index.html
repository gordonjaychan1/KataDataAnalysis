Project Overview

This analysis covers all kata performances recorded across the 2024–25 WKF Karate1 Premier League season and the 2025 World Karate Championships — 9 tournaments total. The four K1 Premier League events in 2024 were held in Paris, Antalya, Cairo, and Casablanca. The four K1 events in 2025 were held in Cairo, Hangzhou, Paris, and Rabat. The 2025 World Championships were held in Abu Dhabi.

Both the Male Kata and Female Kata categories are included. Each row in the dataset represents a single kata performance in a single head-to-head match, recording the kata performed, the average score received, whether the athlete won the match, and which tournament it occurred in.

Data Collection

Data was manually collected from official WKF live scoring sheets and publicly available tournament results. Due to the nature of manual collection, some entries are missing kata names (where the kata performed could not be confirmed from available sources) or scores (where scoring data was incomplete or unavailable).

Only the average score (Avg Score) is retained in this dataset — the raw total score has been dropped. All analyses involving scores use the average score only.

Kata Tier System

Kata in this dataset are classified into three tiers based on their technical complexity and the competitive level at which they are typically introduced:

  • Advanced — High-difficulty kata featuring complex techniques, unusual stances, or rare traditional forms. These are almost exclusively performed at elite international level. Examples: Suparinpei, Unsu, Ohan, Papuren, Gojushiho Sho.
  • Intermediate — Mid-level kata that form the broader competitive repertoire. Common at national and continental level, but also seen occasionally at WKF events. Examples: Sochin, Shisochin, Seienchin, Kanku Dai.
  • Beginner — Lower-difficulty kata rarely seen at this level of competition.

Tier classifications reflect the WKF Approved Kata List difficulty rankings and are applied consistently across both genders.

WKF Scoring System

In WKF kata competition, five judges each assign a score from 0 to 10, evaluating technical performance and athletic performance. The highest and lowest scores from the five judges are discarded, and the remaining three are averaged to produce the final score. This dataset records only the average score (the result after discarding the high and low).

A score above 8.0 is generally considered competitive at the K1 Premier League level. The very top athletes regularly score in the 8.4–9.3 range in finals.

The "Won?" Field

In WKF format, two athletes compete simultaneously in a head-to-head match. Both perform their kata, and the panel of judges indicates who won the match by raising a flag. The Won? field records whether the athlete won this particular head-to-head match. A value of True means they won; False means they lost.

Win rate across multiple performances indicates how often a given kata choice — or athlete — results in a victory. However, win rate is influenced by many factors beyond kata selection: opponent strength, execution quality, judging tendencies, and competition format.

Notes on Interpretation

  • Kata with very few performances (fewer than ~10) may have skewed statistics due to small sample size. Be cautious interpreting averages or win rates for rarely-performed kata.
  • Average score reflects both the difficulty ceiling of the kata and the consistency of athletes who choose to perform it. A kata with a high average score is not necessarily "better" — it may simply be performed by a smaller pool of elite specialists.
  • The male and female events are entirely separate competitions with separate athlete pools. Scores and rankings should not be compared across genders.
  • Some athletes competed in only one or two tournaments; their statistics may not be representative of their full-season performance.
